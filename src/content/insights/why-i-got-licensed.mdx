I've watched Kitchener-Waterloo go through three real-estate cycles. The 2017 craziness, the 2020–2022 free-money distortion, and whatever this current re-balancing turns out to be when we look back at it.

The licensing exam was the easy part. Memorize the Real Estate and Business Brokers Act, work through the Humber courses, sit the test. Done. Anyone with the time and a clear head can pass it.

What's harder — what I'm spending most of my actual energy on — is becoming the kind of realtor I'd hire myself. Patient. Honest. Willing to tell people things they don't want to hear. Willing to spend an extra hour explaining the LTT math instead of getting them to sign. Willing to recommend they wait a year if waiting is what they should do.

That kind of slow, careful pace is rare in real estate. I think it's rare because the industry's commission structure doesn't reward it: agents get paid on close, not on quality-of-advice. The incentive is to move people toward a transaction, fast. I'd like to be different — and I'd rather lose a deal than rush someone into one they'll regret.

What I'm not in a hurry about: hitting volume targets, building a "team", or papering the city with billboards. What I am in a hurry about: getting good at this. Learning each neighborhood deeply, each year, until I know the price history of a block by the address.
