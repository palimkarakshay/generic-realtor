Most renters in Ontario have signed a lease they didn't read carefully. Most landlords prefer it that way. The province actually has a fix for this — a mandatory standard lease form — but if you don't know to ask for it, nobody's going to mention it.

## What it is

The Ontario Standard Lease (Form 2229) is a government-issued residential tenancy agreement. Since April 2018, every new lease for most rental units in Ontario is legally required to use this form. It's plain-language, written by the Ministry of Municipal Affairs and Housing, and free to download.

You can find it on the province's website. Your landlord should already have one.

## What's in it

The form covers the basics in a structured way:

- Names of all tenants and the landlord
- Address of the unit
- Rent amount, due date, and what it covers
- Services included (heat, hydro, water, internet, parking, etc.)
- The list of utilities the *tenant* pays
- Lease term (fixed term, e.g. 12 months, or month-to-month)
- Pet rules (only the *landlord's choice before signing* — see below)
- Smoking rules
- Insurance requirements

The form's Section 15 is where the landlord can add their own additional terms. **Anything outside Section 15 is non-negotiable.** Anything *inside* Section 15 has to be legal — terms that contradict the Residential Tenancies Act are unenforceable, even if you signed them.

## Why landlords who skip it should worry you

A landlord who refuses to use the Ontario Standard Lease is doing one of three things:

1. **They don't know it's required.** Possible — but it means they don't know other tenant-law basics either, and you'll find that out the hard way.
2. **They want to sneak in unenforceable clauses.** "No pets," "no overnight guests," "$500 security deposit on top of last month's rent," "30-day notice from the landlord without cause." All of these are illegal in Ontario, and the Standard Lease makes it obvious they are.
3. **They have something they're hiding.** Maybe the unit isn't technically a legal rental. Maybe they don't want a paper trail.

None of those are reasons to sign their lease.

## What the Standard Lease can't fix

It still doesn't protect you from a landlord who simply ignores their obligations — broken heat in February, refusing repairs, raising rent illegally. For that, you have the Landlord and Tenant Board (LTB). But the Standard Lease gives you the paperwork to win the LTB fight if it comes to one.

## What I'm not certain about

The Standard Lease covers most rental situations but **not all of them**. Care homes, mobile-home parks, social housing, and some types of commercial-residential units have different rules. If you're renting something unusual, double-check with a paralegal.

## What you should do

If a landlord shows you a custom lease, ask: "Can we use the Ontario Standard Lease instead, with your specific terms added in Section 15?" Watch their reaction.

If they push back, walk away. There are other places to rent.
